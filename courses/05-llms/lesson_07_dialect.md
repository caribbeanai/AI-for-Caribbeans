# Lesson 7, Dialect, code switching, and Caribbean language

Our region is multilingual at the level of every street corner. A real product must handle this.

## The languages and dialects

- Standard English. Used in government, education, business.
- Jamaican Patwa (Patois). Lexicon and grammar distinct from English.
- Trinidadian and Tobagonian Creole. Many lexical items from French Patois and Hindi.
- Bajan dialect. Soft consonants, distinctive vowels.
- Vincentian, Grenadian, Saint Lucian, Antiguan, Kittitian creoles.
- Haitian Kreyol. French based, official language.
- Dominican Spanish. Distinct from Caribbean Spanish elsewhere.
- Cuban Spanish.
- Puerto Rican Spanish.
- Curaçao and Aruba Papiamento. Iberian based, with Dutch and African influences.
- Suriname Sranan Tongo. English based creole.
- Belizean Kriol. English based creole.
- Saint Lucian and Dominican Kwéyòl. French based creole spoken alongside English.

## Code switching

A speaker may move between Standard English and creole within a sentence, signalling formality, intimacy, or humour. Models trained on monolingual data lose this signal.

## What to do as a builder

1. Use multilingual models like Aya, NLLB, or large multilingual LLMs for translation and embedding.
2. Build evaluation sets in the target dialect with native speaker review.
3. Avoid forcing creole speakers to type in Standard English. Accept their input as is.
4. For voice products, use ASR with custom vocabularies that include local names.
5. Pay your dialect annotators properly. This work is skilled labour.

## A small dignity rule

Treat each language and dialect as a first class language, not a quirk. Spell its name the way its speakers do. Test your product with real speakers, not just engineers.
