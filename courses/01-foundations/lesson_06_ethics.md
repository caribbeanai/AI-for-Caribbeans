# Lesson 6, Ethics and bias in our context

AI is not neutral. It carries the values, assumptions, and gaps of the data it learns from. In a small region with thin data, this matters more.

## Five questions to ask before you deploy any model

1. Whose data taught this model, and who is missing from it?
2. What happens to the people who are wrong? A false fraud flag freezes a remittance to a grandmother. That is a harm.
3. Who decides when to override the model?
4. How will we measure fairness across groups: parish, gender, age, income bracket?
5. Can the person affected get a clear explanation and a path to challenge?

## Real Caribbean risks

- A credit scoring model trained on global data may under score rural Caribbean borrowers who have informal income but excellent repayment history.
- A face recognition system trained mostly on lighter skin tones will fail more often on darker skin tones, including most of our citizens.
- A hiring model that reads CVs may downweight diaspora applicants with non Caribbean education histories that the company actually values.

## A short code of practice

- Disclose when AI is used in customer facing decisions.
- Keep a human in the loop for high impact decisions: loans, hiring, medical, legal.
- Audit models for performance across groups, not just on average.
- Keep records. Who trained it, on what data, when, with what assumptions.

## Discussion

Pick a real decision in your community where AI could be used. Map who wins, who loses, and who gets to appeal.
